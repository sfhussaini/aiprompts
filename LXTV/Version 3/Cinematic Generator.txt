## Guidelines
### Core Principles
**Be specific and descriptive.**
Instead of "a person walking," write "a young woman in a red coat walking briskly through a rain-soaked Tokyo street at night, neon reflections on wet pavement, handheld camera following from behind." Vague descriptions give the model too many options and produce arbitrary results.
**Describe the full scene.**
Include the subject, their action, the environment, the lighting, and the camera behavior. The more complete the description, the closer the output matches intent.
**Use cinematic language.**
Terms like "full body long shot," "slow dolly in," or "handheld tracking" are understood by the model and directly influence the output. Use them deliberately.
**Describe audio when relevant.**
For endpoints that generate synchronized audio, include audio descriptions: "the sound of rain on pavement," "soft ambient music," "a crowd cheering in the distance."
**Prompt length matters.**
Longer, more descriptive prompts consistently outperform short ones on LTX 2.3. For videos of 8–10 seconds, ensure the prompt is detailed enough to fill the duration. A short prompt for a long video causes the model to rush through the described action.
**Break dialogue into segments.**
When prompting for speaking characters, break long sentences into shorter phrases with acting directions between them. Example:
> A middle-aged man with greying hair speaks in a sad, slow-paced voice, "I remember after you kids came along..." He pauses and looks to the side, then continues, "your mom..." His eyes widen momentarily. He finishes with a cracking voice, "said something to me I never quite understood." The camera slowly zooms into his face. The audio is crisp with faint room tone.
This gives the model explicit direction on pacing, emotion, and physical acting for each beat.
**Audio descriptions have more impact.**
With improved audio quality in LTX 2.3, spend meaningful attention on audio prompts. Describe the acoustic environment, voice qualities, and any ambient sounds desired.
**I2V-Specific: Focus on motion, not the image.**
The visual starting point is already defined by the input image. Describe what happens next — how the subject moves, how the camera follows, what sounds emerge. Do not describe static elements already visible in the image. Describe the transition from stillness to motion.

### What Works Well
**Cinematic compositions** work exceptionally well — wide and long shots with thoughtful lighting, shallow depth of field, and natural motion produce the strongest results.
**Emotive human moments** thrive in LTX 2.3 — strong single-subject emotional expressions, subtle gestures, and facial nuance are rendered with high fidelity.
**Atmosphere and setting** elements such as fog, mist, golden hour light, rain, reflections, and ambient textures consistently elevate the visual quality of generated scenes.
**Clear camera language** scoped within the FULL BODY LONG SHOT constraint gives the model precise direction and reduces interpretive ambiguity.
**Stylized aesthetics** including painterly, noir, analog film, fashion editorial, and pixelated animation are well-supported and produce distinctive results.
**Lighting and mood control** through backlighting, deliberate color palettes, rim light, and flickering lamps gives strong control over scene tone and emotional register.
**Voice capabilities** allow characters to talk and sing, with support for multiple languages and accents.

- Use present tense verbs for all actions and movement.

## Avoid
**Internal emotional states** — Never use abstract labels like "sad," "confused," or "nervous." Express all emotion through physical cues: posture, gesture, facial movement, and pace of breath.
**Text and logos** — Readable text is not currently reliable in LTX 2.3. Avoid prompting for signs, titles, subtitles, or branded elements.
**Complex physics** — Chaotic or physically intricate motion can introduce artifacts. Dancing is acceptable; collapsing structures, explosions, or fluid dynamics are not.
**Overloaded scenes** — Too many characters or simultaneous actions reduce clarity and degrade output quality. Focus on a single subject and a clear action arc per scene.
**Conflicting lighting** — Mixed light logic confuses the model's scene interpretation. Keep all light sources internally consistent within a single prompt.
**Overcomplicated prompts** — Start with a clear, simple core and layer complexity gradually. An overcrowded prompt is harder to execute than a well-prioritized one.
**Describing static image elements** — The input image already defines the visual starting point. Do not re-describe what is already visible. Focus entirely on what changes, moves, or emerges.
**Avoid term 'fabric'** — MUST not use the term fabric. Instead use the clothing layer name.
**Avoid term 'Physical Cue'** — MUST not use the term 'Physical Cue'. Instead use the describe "Facial Expressions/Gestures/Posture/Eye Contact".
**Avoid Semicolons and Colons** — Do NOT use semicolons and colons to create and describe sections. Instead blend the section within single flow description.
**Avoid comma separated lists** — Do NOT use comma-separated tag lists (e.g., "cinematic, golden hour, slow pan").
**Avoid Stable Diffusion or Midjourney Prompts** — Never write in the style of Stable Diffusion or Midjourney prompts.
**Common Mistakes:**
- **Too vague** — "A nice video of nature" gives the model too many options and produces arbitrary output. Be specific about what is in the frame and what moves.
- **Over-constrained** — "Exactly 3 birds flying left to right at 45 degrees while the camera pans right at 2 degrees per second." The model works best with natural language, not numerical specifications.
- **Mismatched duration** — A 10-word prompt for a 10-second video does not give the model enough direction. Long videos need long prompts.
- **Conflicting directions** — "A still, peaceful lake with dramatic waves crashing" creates contradictions that confuse the model. Stay internally consistent.
